Sharing the Library

One person receives, then teaches the next

The library becomes powerful when it moves — when one person saves a useful resource and passes it to someone nearby, and that person does the same. Sharing turns a kit into a multiplying access path.

The goal is not just access. The goal is repeatable sharing. When the first person receives a resource, saves it, and teaches the next person without needing help, the library has done its job — and it will keep doing it long after the team has left.

What makes sharing work

Three things break sharing chains: picking the wrong resource to start with, using a method that's too complicated to remember, and not confirming the new person can repeat it. Getting all three right is the whole task.

  • Start with one file, not the whole library — the most useful resource right now; Scripture chapter, audio Bible, or a film that the community has been asking about
  • Choose a patient, teachable local person — someone who will repeat the process and teach others, not just someone who is nearby
  • Avoid complicated folders and unexplained apps — if they have to figure out why it's in that folder, the chain breaks at the next handoff
  • Leave a printed step guide — the handout is what keeps sharing going after the team leaves; it replaces you

Making sure it keeps moving

  1. Connect or copy to the first device

    Use VillageServer Wi-Fi, microSD, USB, or phone sharing to get one resource onto the first device. Confirm it opens before going any further.

  2. Choose the one most useful file

    Pick the resource most likely to help someone immediately: a Bible chapter in the local language, an audio recording, a gospel film, or a training PDF. One file, clearly chosen.

  3. Save it where they can find it again

    Put it in a folder or app the person can locate without guidance — somewhere they already know, not a new folder with a technical name.

  4. Share it to one nearby person

    Use the simplest transfer method that works for the two phones in front of you. Watch the whole transfer and confirm the second person has the file.

  5. Have the new person share it again

    Ask the second person to share the same file to a third person right now, while you watch. This is the moment sharing becomes their skill, not just a thing you showed them.

  6. Leave the printed handout

    The PDF walks through the same steps in plain language. Keep one with the kit and give one to the person who will keep teaching it forward.

Sharing turns a kit into a multiplying access path. The visiting team is just the first link in the chain.
