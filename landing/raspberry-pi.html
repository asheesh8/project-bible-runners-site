Raspberry Pi VillageServer System

A library that fits in your hand

The Raspberry Pi stores the entire resource library and broadcasts a local Wi-Fi network. Power it on, and any nearby phone can join — no internet, no data plan, no technical setup needed.

The Raspberry Pi is a small computer — about the size of a deck of cards — that stores the entire resource library and broadcasts a private Wi-Fi network. When you power it on in the field, it becomes a wireless library that any nearby phone can join without a data plan.

Think of it this way

When someone joins the VillageServer Wi-Fi on their phone, it feels exactly like connecting to Wi-Fi at a coffee shop. They tap a link, a browser opens, and they're browsing the library. The difference is that the library is sitting right in front of them — on a device the size of a credit card — and it works whether there's internet or not.

One Pi can serve multiple phones at the same time, making it ideal for group settings: churches, schools, training workshops, or community gatherings. People read, listen, download files, and share them to other phones — all without any of it touching the internet.

How a session works

The whole process takes under two minutes once someone is trained. Here's what it looks like from power-on to first file:

  1. Plug in power

    Use a wall outlet, charged battery, or solar-supported source. The Pi draws very little current — a standard battery pack runs it for several hours.

  2. Wait for the network

    The Pi takes about 60 seconds to boot. The VillageServer Wi-Fi name appears on nearby phones when it's ready.

  3. Connect a phone

    Go to Wi-Fi settings and join the VillageServer network. No password needed unless the kit is configured for one.

  4. Open the library

    Tap the address shown on the included card, or wait for the captive portal to appear automatically in the browser.

  5. Find a resource

    Browse to a Bible chapter, audio file, gospel film, or PDF. The library is organized by Read, Listen, Watch, and Children.

  6. Save and share

    Download the file so it stays on the phone after the server is packed away. Then share it to another phone using AirDrop, Quick Share, or LocalSend.

What it can do

  • Serve 5–15 phones simultaneously — the whole group connects at once
  • Run from battery for hours — no wall power needed in the field
  • Store a large library — audio, video, PDFs, and full Bible text
  • Work completely offline — no internet required at any point during use
  • Load custom content — libraries can be configured with specific languages and resources before deployment
