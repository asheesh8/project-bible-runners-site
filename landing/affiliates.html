Ministry Resource Partners

Stronger together than alone

VillageServer doesn't pretend to do everything by itself. Trusted partners provide content, field relationships, hardware support, and credibility that make the initiative more effective in every setting.

Good field work is built on trust — trust in the content, trust in the organization behind it, and trust earned over time through real relationships. VillageServer is stronger because of the partners who provide the resources, connections, and accountability that no single initiative can generate on its own.

Who we partner with and why

Each partner fills a specific gap. VillageServer focuses on the technology layer — the device, the library, the transfer process. Partners bring the content, relationships, and access that make that technology useful in specific communities.

  • Project Bible Runners — field relationships and Scripture-access context; the mission connections that open doors VillageServer couldn't reach alone
  • Digital Bible Society — curated digital Bible resources, pre-built library content, and established relationships with Bible translation and distribution networks
  • TechSoup — discounted nonprofit technology hardware, software licensing, and sourcing support that keeps kit costs lower for small mission organizations

How to work with partners well

  1. Name the specific need first

    Decide whether the gap is content, hardware, field access, training materials, nonprofit support, or translation before reaching out to a partner. A clear request gets a faster answer.

  2. Match the right partner to the need

    Don't route every request to every partner. Project Bible Runners for field relationships, Digital Bible Society for content libraries, TechSoup for hardware discounts.

  3. Make a specific, concrete request

    Ask for the specific resource, license permission, discount, or introduction needed — not a general "can you help?" conversation.

  4. Credit partners accurately

    Keep partner names, content permissions, and licensing terms visible and up-to-date in any public-facing materials or board reports.

  5. Review annually

    Update partner relationships as the initiative grows. New field settings may require new partnerships; old arrangements may need renewal or renegotiation.

Affiliates make the initiative more credible and less isolated. The goal isn't independence — it's a trusted network that multiplies reach.
